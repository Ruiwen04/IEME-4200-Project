The Challenge

NYC is one of the most transient cities in the world — and one of the loneliest.

Our team explored how urban change and urban density leave millions of neighbors who never become a neighborhood.

"I see the same people every day — but I don't know them. Belonging often requires already belonging."

— Interview participant, NYC resident

How might we help New York City residents build real, lasting community connections — despite the transient, fast-moving nature of city life?

Design Process

From territory to solution — what I did at each step.

I followed the HCDI human-centered design framework across 14 weeks, working collaboratively at every step to understand why community is so hard to build in a transient city — and what could change that.

01
Territory Research

I helped research the territory of urban transience and community loss in NYC — reading through studies on social isolation, neighborhood turnover, and how city residents struggle to form lasting connections. I also contributed to writing up the findings in our research document.

02
Customer Interviews

I conducted one interview with a student living in New York City, exploring how they experienced their neighborhood, whether they felt a sense of community, and what made it hard to connect with people around them in such a fast-moving city. I also helped write up the interview findings as part of our shared research document.

Interview designMulti-borough outreachResidents & small businesses
03
Insight Development

Together we distilled a core insight: people don't lack the desire to connect — they lack repeated, structured reasons to interact with the same people. In a transient city, one-time encounters are the norm. Only consistent, recurring encounters build real trust. I contributed to writing this up in our shared documents.

Ideation

Three ideas. One chosen.

We developed three distinct concepts before stakeholder evaluation pointed us toward our final direction.

🏪
Idea 01
Space Matching Platform
Connects landlords with vacant retail spaces to artists and small businesses. An interactive NYC map shows available spaces in real-time, with a vetting system that prioritizes independent artists over large chains.
📱
Idea 02
Digital Cultural Archive
A platform where residents document and support cultural spaces before they disappear — featuring an interactive cultural map, storytelling tools, and partnership opportunities for neighborhood pop-ups and tours.
🎪
Idea 03 · Selected
Community Hub Network
A network of neighborhood cultural hubs across NYC, run by the community for the community. An app handles all bookings and ticketing. Revenue stays 100% local — zero platform cut. Every culture gets a seat.
✓ Chosen based on stakeholder feedback
📊

Why Idea 3 Won

No existing platform scored well on both repeated interaction and open accessibility. Stakeholder ratings confirmed the gap: Idea 3 was the only concept that could realistically deliver both.

Evaluation

Validated by real stakeholders.

We used the rating-and-explanation technique with multiple stakeholders — then let the feedback decide.

👥
Who We Talked To
Long-time NYC residents, small business owners, artists, and community organizers across multiple boroughs who directly experience neighborhood disconnection.
📊
What We Found
We gathered ratings and explanations across all three ideas and looked for patterns — finding that "repeated" was what stakeholders valued most. That became our core design principle.
💬
Key Feedback Themes
Stakeholders consistently valued repeated interactions over one-time events, and prioritized affordability and community ownership over platform-controlled solutions.
✅
The Decision
Evidence — not the team's preferences — pointed to the community hub model. We refined it into Next on the Block, with repeat encounters as the engine of community resilience.

The Solution

Next on the Block

An app that uses local events as a repeated reason to show up — so that in one of the world's most transient cities, strangers can actually become neighbors.

Building community before it's needed.

Next on the Block connects NYC residents through hyper-local cultural events. The key word is repeat — the same vendors, the same neighbors, the same block. Recurring encounters are what turn a transient city into a community.

🗺️
Borough-Based Discovery

Find events happening on your block across all 5 NYC boroughs.

🎟️
One-Tap Ticketing

Buy a ticket, see the subway line, show up. That simple.

🗳️
Community Governance

Residents vote on what events happen next. Every culture gets a seat.

🛍️
Artist & Vendor Launchpad

Local creatives build a following through recurring cultural merch drops.

Reflection

What this semester taught me.

Four things I'll carry forward — as an engineer, a researcher, and a designer.

📊
Data Needs a Human Story
I came in comfortable with numbers and analysis. What I learned is that data gets you to the door — but a conversation with one real person can open it. Our best insight didn't come from census data; it came from a resident saying "belonging requires already belonging."
🎯
Evidence Over Intuition
As an engineer I'm trained to find the right answer. Design taught me to let customers define what "right" means. We abandoned our first instinct — a cultural archive tool — because interviews showed people needed connection, not documentation.
🔄
Iteration Is the Method
The final solution didn't exist in week one — it emerged through three concepts, stakeholder feedback, and willingness to let go. For someone trained to plan precisely, learning to design iteratively was genuinely uncomfortable and genuinely valuable.
🌆
Design Has Real Stakes
This problem is real — people in NYC genuinely feel isolated and disconnected from those around them. Working on something with genuine human stakes made every design choice feel like it mattered. That's a standard I want to hold onto.
